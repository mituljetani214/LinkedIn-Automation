## Natural Voice

- How do you usually explain design?
  I explain design through simple, real-world examples. I prefer to make the problem clear first, then explain the solution in a neat, structured, and easy-to-understand way. For me, design is not just about showing screens or visual polish. It is about helping people understand what problem exists, why it matters, and how the solution improves the experience.

- Do you prefer simple, technical, storytelling, or tactical writing?
  I prefer simple and minimal writing first. The style should depend on the topic. Some topics need storytelling, some need tactical advice, and some need technical depth, but the final explanation should always feel clear, useful, and easy to understand.

## Your Stories

- First design lesson:
  Early in my career, I used to design without fully understanding the user, target audience, or real problem. That taught me an important lesson: never jump directly into design. Before designing, do proper research, understand the actual problem, and validate what users and the business really need. Good design starts with discovery, not decoration.

- Biggest design mistake:
  A major mistake is copying another website, product, or competitor without understanding why they made those decisions. Inspiration is useful, but blindly cloning a solution can damage the product because the context, users, business model, and goals may be completely different. A designer should learn from others, then design for their own product's actual needs.

- Best project win:
  To be completed with a specific project story. Recommended options: Munim invoice workflow, Munim product growth period, DiamondRensu CRO improvement, or design system implementation.

- Hard client/stakeholder moment:
  Stakeholder decision-making becomes difficult when too many people are involved and everyone has a different opinion. The best way to handle this is not to argue from preference, but to bring research, user cases, evidence, and clear options to the table. When the problem and evidence are visible, decisions become easier and more aligned.

- Career turning point:
  Figma became a major turning point because it changed how I could think and present design at the architecture level. It gave me a globally used tool to solve real product problems, build systems, collaborate better, and explain design decisions more clearly. Learning Figma deeply helped me move from visual design toward product design, UX strategy, and scalable design systems.

- Why UI/UX matters to you:
  UI/UX matters because building a product without UX is like building a house without architecture. You may have an idea in your mind, but without planning, structure, research, and a clear base, the result can waste time and money. UX gives the product its foundation. It helps teams understand the problem before they build the solution.

## Tone Settings

- Confidence level: Proactive, clear, and grounded
- Humor level: 50%, used lightly when it makes the topic easier to understand
- Personal story level: 20%, enough to feel human without making every post about personal experience
- Technical depth: Medium, practical, and explained through examples
- Recruiter focus: 40%, show leadership, product impact, AI workflow ability, and business outcomes
- Designer community focus: 40%, teach practical lessons about UX, product thinking, design systems, and AI-assisted design

## Writing Rules For Future Content

- Start with a real problem, observation, or lesson.
- Explain design through simple examples whenever possible.
- Keep the message practical and useful.
- Connect design decisions to user value and business impact.
- Use AI as a practical workflow advantage, not as hype.
- Avoid claiming personal ownership of outcomes that were team or company outcomes.
- Prefer clear language over clever language.
- Make posts useful to product designers, UX designers, founders, recruiters, and product teams.
- Keep the tone confident, humble, direct, and human.
